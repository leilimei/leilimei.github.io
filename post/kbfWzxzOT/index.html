A chemical strategy is expected to lead to better peptide drugs

2020-04-24

Summary:

Ross cheloha, a graduate student at the University of Wisconsin, and his mentor Sam Gellman, working with researchers at Harvard Medical School, built a parathyroid hormone version by replacing the skeleton of the synthetic hormone with an artificial unit, which is resistant to degradation in laboratory mice. Professor Gellman said that the changed hormone can stay longer at higher concentrations. Relevant research results were published in Nature Biotechnology on June 15, 2014.

Hormones are signaling molecules that are distributed throughout the body, usually in the blood. Hormones respond only in cells that carry the right receptor molecules. "The receptor has evolved to recognize a very specific signal in a large body fluid filled with molecular information," Gellman said

The relationship between receptors and their signaling molecules is often compared to a lock and a key.

Gellman said: "this result makes us very excited, because we can maintain the ability to activate the receptor by changing the skeleton of hormone, and maintain the necessary contact point at the right position. At the same time, we have preserved, or even enhanced, the signal capacity, and we have reduced the susceptibility of peptides to the biodegradation mechanism that nature uses over time to clear signals.

Peptides are protein fragments. Peptide hormones, like the familiar steroid hormones like estrogen and testosterone, can send a signal to billions of cells at the same time, even in very small concentrations.

In this study, the researchers modified a very successful synthetic parathyroid hormone (called tripeptide), which is used to treat severe osteoporosis.

But what's really exciting about this discovery is its potential impact on a wide range of peptide drugs. "A lot of receptors, including some involved in diabetes, respond to peptide signals, but peptides degrade quickly in the body," Gellman said. Our approach proposes a general strategy to maintain the ability to target a specific receptor while reducing the role of degrading enzymes. Most importantly, the receptor is looking for a shape, while the destroying enzyme is looking for a different shape. "

"The idea of replacing fragments of peptide skeletons with artificial units used to seem heretical," Gellman said. Most people expect that you will not be able to change the skeleton, which will change the space and direction of the necessary contact points so that the molecules are recognized by the receptor. "

The strategy of "changing the skeleton" allows for the oral administration of rather vulnerable peptide drugs, which today must be administered by injection to avoid damage in the stomach and small intestine. By protecting the drug from degrading enzymes, the new method can also help maintain high drug concentrations in the blood.

Moreover, the changed skeleton seems to make only minor changes in the signal, once activated, the receptor will transfer it to the cell. "Changing where the skeleton is modified can lead to different reactions," Gellman said. The preparation of drug molecules that activate only certain types of reactions allows us to avoid unwanted side effects. "

So far, most of the drug development has focused on the external characteristics of signal molecules, which directly contact the receptor. "The traditional approach is to keep the skeleton the same and modify the surface composition," Gellman said. Our approach is the opposite, maintaining the same surface composition and modifying the skeleton. Because it's clear that this unconventional approach can succeed, it's possible that others will try it as well. "
