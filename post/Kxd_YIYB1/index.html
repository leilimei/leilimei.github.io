The seven facts of glycosylated hemoglobin

Summary:

  1. What is glycosylated hemoglobin test?

Glycosylated hemoglobin (HbA1c) is formed by hemoglobin, the oxygen carrying protein contained in the red blood cells of blood, which is wrapped by blood glucose. The amount of glucose bound to hemoglobin is directly proportional to the total glucose content in the blood system, so the higher the blood glucose level, the higher the HbA1c level. Red blood cells live only 120 days, so by measuring HbA1c, doctors can determine the average blood sugar level over a period of about 2-3 months.

HbA1c is particularly important for diabetics, because the higher the HbA1c level, the greater the risk of diabetic complications. HbA1c was also used to assess the effectiveness of treatment regimens in the control of blood glucose levels after the diagnosis of diabetes.

  1. What is the difference between glycosylated hemoglobin test and blood glucose test?

HbA1c does not need to be fasting. It can reflect the average blood glucose level of 2-3 months. It is a more stable test for evaluating the effect of long-term blood glucose control. This means that glycosylated hemoglobin level is not easy to produce daily fluctuations caused by pressure and disease, while blood glucose detection only represents the blood glucose concentration in the blood at that time.

3、 What is the target glycosylated hemoglobin?

For most patients who have been diagnosed with diabetes mellitus, HbA1c level less than 7% is a common treatment goal, but doctors should customize individual goals for patients. The HbA1c value does not reflect a specific diabetes complication, which means that any level of HbA1c examination value can produce any complication. Of course, the closer the glycosylated hemoglobin test value is to the normal value, the better.

  1. What are the benefits of reducing glycosylated hemoglobin?

Studies have shown that if HbA1c levels in diabetics are reduced by 1%, severe complications in the eyes, kidneys, and nervous system will be reduced by 25%.

In addition, a study published in the journal BMJ showed that patients with type 2 diabetes had a 1% reduction in HbA1c levels: a 19% reduction in the risk of cataracts; a 16% reduction in the risk of heart failure; and a 43% reduction in the risk of amputation or death due to vascular disease.

Therefore, a lower HbA1c level means a lower mean blood glucose level and a lower risk of complications.

  1. Who should have a glycosylated hemoglobin test?

① HbA1c should be tested at least once a year for every type 2 diabetic.

② Some patients may need more frequent HbA1c tests, especially in patients with recent changes in treatment regimens, or when medical staff need to monitor patients' condition more frequently.

③ For people who don't have diabetes, experts suggest that people over the age of 45 should consider glycosylated hemoglobin tests, especially those with overweight / obesity. If you are under 45 but overweight and have one or more additional diabetes risk factors, you should also consider a test.

  1. What are the limitations of glycosylated hemoglobin test?

Although HbA1c is generally reliable and widely used, it may not be accurate in the following populations:

① Reduction of hemoglobin due to excessive blood loss (which may result in low HbA1c detection value);

② Iron deficiency anemia (may cause HbA1c detection value to be too high);

③ Hemoglobin gene mutation or abnormal hemoglobin: common in African American, Mediterranean or Southeast Asian people;

④ Recent history of blood transfusion or other forms of hemolytic anemia (may cause the HbA1c test result to be too low);

⑤ Pregnancy.

  1. How to control glycosylated hemoglobin level?

Anyone can control HbA1c level by adopting healthy lifestyle, which will benefit people to reduce long-term diabetic complications (such as heart disease, stroke, renal failure and diabetic neuropathic pain). To reduce the level of glycosylated hemoglobin, efforts should be made to:

Healthy diet:

Diabetics and prediabetes need to eat low heat and nutritious foods, such as fruits, vegetables, dietary fiber, high-quality protein and appropriate amount of monounsaturated / polyunsaturated fatty acids; meanwhile, the intake of saturated fatty acids, carbohydrates and deep processed foods should be limited.

For example, eating whole wheat bread and brown rice instead of white bread and white rice will help reduce peak blood sugar after meals. If necessary, consult a nutritionist for food selection and diet planning; you can also use a daily diet diary or calorie counter to track your daily food intake.

Exercise regularly:

By sticking to exercise, blood sugar will be transferred from the blood to the cells to generate energy for the body, thus reducing blood sugar levels. In addition, exercise can improve the body's sensitivity to insulin.

Everyone should bring physical activities into their daily life. For people without diabetes, exercise will help to prevent the occurrence of prodromal diabetes and type 2 diabetes; for people with diabetes, exercise can help to maintain a good level of blood sugar.

The American Diabetes Association recommends at least five days a week, 30 minutes a day, or 150 minutes a week, of moderate to high-intensity aerobic exercise (moderate intensity refers to the extent that you can talk normally but can't sing while exercising, while high intensity means you can't talk normally without pausing).

Maintain a healthy weight:

If the patient is overweight, they can try to lose weight through diet and exercise, which will significantly reduce blood sugar level and HbA1c value.

Regular monitoring:

It is generally recommended that glycosylated hemoglobin be monitored for 2-3 months to assess diabetes control. However, HbA1c test can not completely replace blood glucose monitoring. For example, patients treated with insulin and other drugs need regular blood glucose monitoring to ensure that blood glucose is not too low.
