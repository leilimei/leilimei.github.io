Lu Lu / Jiang Shibo / Qinchuan team jointly developed anti HIV peptide with new mechanism

2020-07-23

Institute of Microbiology, Chinese Academy of Sciences

At present, cocktail therapy with multiple drugs has changed HIV infection from a fatal disease to a chronic disease with little impact on life expectancy. However, the clinical use of HIV drugs is still facing the challenge of emerging HIV resistant strains. Almost all drugs can induce drug-resistant strains in a few months to several years under the condition of single drug use. HIV inhibitors targeting new targets are expected to be used to treat HIV resistant strains infected with existing drugs.

More importantly, the current HIV drugs can not cure HIV and need to be taken for life. Among the ways to cure HIV, "shock and kill" is one of the most basic research strategies. This strategy mainly through the activation of HIV latent reservoir cells, and then kill the activated infected cells and viruses, but at present, there is still a lack of HIV drugs that can directly kill the infected cells and viruses at the same time.

In view of the above problems, Lulu / Jiang Shibo team of Fudan University and Qinchuan team of Chinese Academy of Medical Sciences published an article on Science Translational Medicine on June 3, 2020, targeting the gp41cytotoxic tail kills HIV-1 viruses and infected cells, and screened out anti HIV peptides targeting new targets.

The peptide, f9170, is derived from the intracellular domain of HIV envelope protein (Env), and can effectively inhibit and inactivate HIV infection, that is, it can make the virus lose the ability to infect the target cells before the virus infects the target cells, which is significantly different from the drugs that need to bind or even enter the replication cycle. Further mechanism studies showed that f9170 targeted the llp1 domain of HIV envelope protein, and the interaction between peptide and env intracellular domain formed a hydrophilic channel on the viral envelope, which eventually resulted in the release of HIV genome and loss of infectivity (Fig. 1).

The peptide can also act on HIV infected cells expressing HIV Env and activated HIV repository, resulting in necrosis of infected / activated cells. Therefore, f9170 may be used in the treatment of HIV infection together with repository activator.

The safety test of f9170 in mice showed that the peptide did not affect the weight of mice, did not cause hepatorenal toxicity and neurotoxicity, and did not induce anti-f9170 antibody. In the rhesus monkey model with chronic infection of SHIV, the short-term use of f9170 alone can quickly control the viral load in rhesus monkeys below the detection line. At the same time, the half-life of f9170 in rhesus monkey can reach 8-11 hours, which is higher than the previous peptide drugs.

In addition, drug signals can be detected in the brain and lymph nodes of mice in vivo distribution experiment, and these two organs are currently recognized as the location of HIV latent pool. Therefore, f9170 is expected to be a candidate drug for HIV treatment and even cure. The mechanism of action of f9170 is different from the previous drugs. It does not damage the physiological function of the target, but uses the interaction with the target to destroy the integrity of the cell membrane, which broadens the thinking for the development of other viral drugs.
