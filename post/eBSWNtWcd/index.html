JAMA inter Med: have you eaten all the protein these years? Eating more protein may not be good for the elderly!

2020-04-22

Summary:

Regardless of gender and age, the daily intake of protein recommended by doctors is the same: 0.8-1g/kg/day. At the same time, many experts and national institutions recommend that the elderly eat more protein to maintain muscle growth. But few studies have explored the benefits of more protein intake in the elderly. A randomized clinical study conducted by Dr. shalender Bhasin, a researcher at BWh, and colleagues, found that more protein intake in the elderly did not increase the quality and function of muscle tissue. Their research results were recently published in JAMA internal medicine.

"Surprisingly, there's not a lot of evidence to support just how much protein we need," he said Dr Bhasin, co-author of the study, said he is the team leader of the men's health project in BWh's aging and metabolism department. "Despite the lack of evidence, experts recommend that older people eat more protein. We want to do a rigorous test of this situation and determine whether eating more protein can increase muscle mass, strength and function. "

This clinical study is called optimizing protein intake in old men trial (optimization), which is a randomized, placebo-controlled, double-blind parallel trial. Men aged 65 and over were randomly divided into four groups, and the following experiments were carried out respectively: Taking protein according to 0.8-g / kg / day and receiving blank injection; Protein was taken in accordance with 1.3-g / kg / day and received blank injection; protein was taken in accordance with 0.8-g / kg / day and testosterone was injected once a week; protein was taken in accordance with 1.3-g / kg / day and testosterone was injected once a week. Each participant ate packaged food containing personalized protein, energy components and supplements. A total of 78 participants completed the six-month experiment.

The team found that higher protein intake did not significantly increase muscle mass, fat mass, muscle function, physiological function, etc.

"Our data highlights the need to reassess the recommended daily protein intake guidelines for older people, especially those who are weak or have chronic disease."

reference material:

Shalender Bhasin et al, Effect of Protein Intake on Lean Body Mass in Functionally Limited Older Men, JAMA Internal Medicine (2018). DOI: 10.1001/jamainternmed.2018.0008
