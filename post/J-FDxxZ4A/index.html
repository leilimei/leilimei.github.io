Biological coupling of polypeptide drugs

2020-01-13

Biocoupling is a chemical strategy to form a stable covalent bond between two molecules, at least one of which is a biomolecule. The field of bio coupling has a significant impact on the production of academic research, clinical diagnosis and therapeutics.

Polypeptides are a kind of important molecules in bio coupling and derivatization. Peptide drug conjugates have obvious advantages in improving the potential of drug therapy and have broad prospects in drug delivery. Both linear and cyclic peptides are considered to be transporters because they are easy to synthesize, simple in structure and low in immunogenicity.

Targeted drug delivery

Research is under way to develop new drug delivery systems for targeted drug delivery. The peptide extracted from the cell surface protein sequence has a strong binding affinity to the target cell surface receptor. The combination of peptide drug conjugates with specific ligands such as EGF and RGD Peptides can provide a solution for selective targeted drug delivery.

Chemistry:

Drugs and bioactive molecules are coupled with chemical reaction groups (such as amines, mercaptans, carboxylic acids, hydroxyl groups, aldehydes and ketones), active hydrogen, photochemistry and cycloaddition reactions, zero length cross-linking, homofunctional, heterofunctional or multifunctional cross-linking chemistry, dendritic molecules and dendrites as well as cleavable reagent systems.

The chemical process of biological coupling may occur through the carbon and nitrogen ends of each polypeptide chain, the carboxyl group of aspartic acid and glutamic acid, the amino group of lysine, the guanidine group of arginine, the sulfhydryl group of cysteine, the hydroxy group of tyrosine phenol, the indole ring of tryptophan, the Thioether Group of methionine and the imidazole ring of histidine.

Procedure:

All customized biomolecular synthesis, modification or biological conjugation services are produced under strict quality control. The final target coupling compound must first be separated from the excess or unreacted reagents. In many cases, simple dialysis is enough to remove unreacted reagents from the reaction solution. HPLC can also be used to remove redundant reagents or to separate and characterize cross-linked products. The target molecules can be further characterized by biochemical or biophysical techniques.
