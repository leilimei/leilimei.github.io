Peptides are complex macromolecules, so each sequence is unique in physical and chemical properties. Some peptides are difficult to synthesize, while others are relatively easy to synthesize but difficult to purify. The most common problem is that many peptides are insoluble in aqueous solution, so in purification, these hydrophobic peptides must be soluble in non-aqueous solvent, or special buffer, which may not be suitable for biological experimental system, so researchers can not use the peptide to achieve their own purposes, so the following are some suggestions for researchers to design peptides.

Selection of difficult peptide

  1. The reduction of sequence length is due to the increase of peptide length, which leads to the decrease of purity of crude product. Peptide with less than 15 residues can be easily obtained. When the length of peptide chain increases to more than 20 residues, the amount of correct product is a major consideration. In many experiments, it is often better to reduce the residual number below 20.

  2. It is difficult to reduce the dominant peptide, especially in the region 7-12 residues from the C-terminal. This is generally believed to be due to the formation of β - folded sheets in synthesis, which results in incomplete coupling. In these examples, it may be helpful to replace with one or more polar residues, or to add Gly or pro to open the peptide structure.

  3. It is usually difficult to synthesize Cys, met, Arg, try residues to reduce "difficult" residues. Ser is usually used as a non oxidative replacement of Cys.

Selection for improved solubility

  1. Change N-terminal or C-terminal to acid peptide (i.e. pH value is 7 with negative charge), we recommend acetylation (N-terminal acetylation, C-terminal keeps free carboxyl) to increase negative charge. For basic peptides (i.e. with positive charge at pH 7), we recommend acylation (N-terminal free amino group, C-terminal acylation) to increase the positive charge.

  2. There are a lot of hydrophobic amino acids in some sequences of shortened or lengthened sequences, such as Trp, Phe, Val, ile, Leu, met, Tyr and Ala. when these hydrophobic residues are more than 50%, they are usually difficult to dissolve. In order to increase the polarity of peptides, lengthening the sequence may be helpful. Another option is to increase polarity by reducing the length of the peptide chain by reducing the hydrophobic residues. The higher the polarity of the peptide chain, the more likely it is to dissolve in water.

  3. For some peptide chains, adding some polar amino acids can improve the solubility. We recommend adding Glu Glu to the N-terminal or C-terminal of acid peptide. Add Lys Lys to the N-terminal or C-terminal of the basic peptide. If the charged group cannot be added, Ser Gly ser can be added to the N-terminal or C-terminal. However, this method is not feasible when the two ends of the peptide chain cannot be changed.

  4. Changing the solubility of the peptide chain by replacing one or more residues can be improved by changing some residues in the sequence. In general, the substitution of a single residue can significantly improve its hydrophobicity, which is usually conservative, such as the substitution of Gly for ala.

  5. By selecting different "frames" to change the sequence, if a certain sequence can be used to prepare many peptides with a certain length, which are connected or overlapped with each other, the method of changing the starting point of each peptide can be used to achieve the purpose of changing the sequence.

The principle is to create a new and better balance between the hydrophilic and hydrophobic residues of the same polypeptide, or to put the "difficult" residues (such as 2 Cys) in the same polypeptide into two different polypeptides instead of the same molecule.
